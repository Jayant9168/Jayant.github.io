01
Foundation
ACLS Overview & Chain of Survival

Advanced Cardiovascular Life Support (ACLS) is a set of clinical protocols for the urgent treatment of cardiac arrest, stroke, and other life-threatening emergencies. ACLS builds upon Basic Life Support (BLS) and incorporates medication delivery, advanced airway management, and systematic rhythm interpretation.

🚨
Core Principle High-quality CPR remains the single most important intervention in cardiac arrest. No drug or device replaces excellent compressions and early defibrillation.

In-Hospital Chain of Survival (AHA 2020)

👁️
1. Surveillance & Prevention

Identify deteriorating patients early using rapid response teams and early warning scores (NEWS2, MEWS).

📢
2. Recognition & Activation

Immediate recognition of cardiac arrest; activation of the emergency response system (Code Blue).

🫀
3. High-Quality CPR

Rate 100–120/min, depth 5–6 cm, full recoil, minimal interruptions, 30:2 ratio.

⚡
4. Rapid Defibrillation

Shock shockable rhythms (VF/pVT) as quickly as possible. Every minute without shock decreases survival by 7–10%.

💊
5. Advanced Resuscitation

Medications, advanced airway, IV/IO access, rhythm-directed treatment.

🏥
6. Post-Arrest Care

Targeted temperature management, PCI, hemodynamic optimization, neuroprognostication.

Out-of-Hospital Chain of Survival

🏘️ Out-of-Hospital Steps

1. Recognition and call 9-1-1  →  2. Bystander CPR  →  3. AED deployment (< 3–5 min)  →  4. EMS arrival and ACLS  →  5. Advanced hospital care  →  6. Recovery

02
Primary Assessment
BLS Survey & Initial Assessment

BLS Survey (Primary Survey)

1
Safety — Scene Safety

Ensure environment is safe for rescuers. Don PPE. Identify hazards (electrical, chemical, trauma).

2
Stimulate — Check Responsiveness

Tap shoulders firmly, shout "Are you okay?" If no response → unresponsive.

3
Shout for Help — Activate EMS

Call for code team / 9-1-1. Send someone for AED. In hospital: pull crash cart, activate Code Blue.

4
Circulation — Check Pulse

Carotid pulse ≤ 10 seconds. If no pulse (or unsure) → begin CPR immediately. No pulse check should delay compressions.

5
Airway — Open & Assess

Head-tilt chin-lift (or jaw-thrust if trauma). Look for visible obstruction. Clear if necessary.

6
Breathing — Rescue Breaths

2 rescue breaths via BVM at 1 second each. Observe chest rise. Avoid hyperventilation. 30:2 ratio with compressions.

7
Defibrillation — AED/Manual

Apply pads as soon as available. Analyze rhythm. Shock if advised. Minimize pre/post-shock pauses (< 10 sec).

05
Non-Shockable Rhythms
PEA & Asystole Algorithm

Pulseless Electrical Activity (PEA) and Asystole are non-shockable rhythms. Do NOT shock. Focus on high-quality CPR, epinephrine, and identifying/treating reversible causes (H's & T's).

Pulseless Electrical Activity (PEA)

Organized electrical activity on monitor without a palpable pulse. ECG may look normal, but no cardiac output. Must actively search for reversible causes.

Asystole ("Flatline")

Absence of any electrical activity. Confirm in two leads. Rarely survivable unless cause is rapidly reversible (e.g., hypothermia). Pseudo-asystole must be excluded.

Confirming Asystole

1
Check connections and leads

Loose leads can mimic asystole. Verify pad contact and monitor cables.

2
Confirm in two leads

True asystole must be flat in at least two separate ECG leads (e.g., Lead II and Lead V1).

3
Increase gain

Turn up ECG gain to maximum to detect any fine VF that might be treatable with defibrillation.

Emergent PCI is indicated for STEMI on post-ROSC ECG. For non-ST-elevation arrest without obvious non-cardiac cause, early coronary angiography is reasonable but not mandatory. Shared decision-making with cardiology is key.

Stable Narrow-Complex Tachycardia (< 0.12 sec)

1
Vagal Maneuvers (1st Line)

Valsalva (modified: supine, 40 mmHg for 15 sec then legs elevated); carotid sinus massage (one side, < 10 sec); diving reflex (cold water face immersion).

2
Adenosine (SVT / Regular)

6 mg rapid IV push (antecubital or larger vein) with 20 mL NS flush. If no conversion → 12 mg (may repeat 12 mg once). Warn patient of chest pressure, flushing, brief asystole.

3
Rate Control (Atrial Fibrillation — Irregular)

Diltiazem 15–20 mg IV over 2 min; metoprolol 2.5–5 mg IV every 5 min (max 3 doses); or amiodarone for AF with WPW.

Stable Wide-Complex Tachycardia (≥ 0.12 sec)

⚡ Default Rule

Treat ALL wide-complex tachycardia as Ventricular Tachycardia (VT) until proven otherwise. VT is the most common cause. Do not assume SVT with aberrancy.

Systematic ECG Interpretation — 8-Step Method

1
Rate

300 ÷ large boxes between R-R (regular rhythm). Or: 1500 ÷ small boxes. Or: count beats in 6-second strip × 10.

2
Rhythm

Regular or irregular? If irregular → regularly irregular (pattern) or irregularly irregular (AF)?

3
P Waves

Present? Morphology? Upright in II? Inverted in aVR? One P per QRS? Relationship to QRS?

4
PR Interval

Measure in Lead II. Normal 120–200 ms. Fixed or variable?

5
QRS Duration

Narrow (< 120 ms) = supraventricular origin. Wide (≥ 120 ms) = BBB or ventricular origin.

6
ST Segment

Elevation or depression? In which leads? STE in II, III, aVF = inferior STEMI. V1–V4 = anterior STEMI.

7
T Waves

Morphology, direction, symmetry. Hyperacute T waves may precede STE. Wellens' pattern = LAD stenosis.

8
QT Interval / Axis

Calculate QTc. Determine axis (normal: –30° to +90°). LAD or RAD significance.

💡 If Unknown Device Capability

If the biphasic energy setting is unknown, use 200 J as the default starting dose. Always use the highest available energy for subsequent shocks if first shock fails.

Synchronized Cardioversion

The defibrillator delivers a shock synchronized to the R wave (to avoid delivering energy during the T wave vulnerable period, which can induce VF). Used for organized rhythms with a pulse.

⚠️
Cardioversion — Remember Anticoagulation For AF/flutter > 48 hours (or unknown duration), perform TEE to exclude LAA thrombus before elective cardioversion, or anticoagulate for ≥ 3 weeks prior. In hemodynamic instability, cardiovert immediately regardless of duration.

Transcutaneous Pacing (TCP)

1
Indications

Symptomatic bradycardia unresponsive to atropine; complete heart block; overdose-induced bradycardia; bridge to transvenous pacing.

2
Pad Placement

Anterior-posterior position preferred (anterior left precordial / posterior left infrascapular). Standard defib pad placement is acceptable.

3
Set Rate & Output

Set rate at 60–80 bpm. Start output at 0 mA, increase by 10 mA increments until electrical capture (wide QRS after each pacing spike) is seen — typically 40–80 mA, up to 200 mA.

4
Confirm Mechanical Capture

Palpate femoral or brachial pulse with each QRS. Do not rely solely on monitor artifact. SpO₂ plethysmography can help confirm mechanical capture.

5
Sedation & Analgesia

TCP is painful. Administer midazolam 1–2 mg + fentanyl 1 mcg/kg IV for awake patients as soon as feasible.

Reversible Causes
The H's and T's

Every cardiac arrest should prompt a systematic search for reversible causes. The "H's and T's" is a mnemonic for the most common treatable etiologies. Treating these is the cornerstone of PEA management.

🔬 POCUS in Cardiac Arrest

Point-of-care ultrasound during cardiac arrest (performed without interrupting CPR) can identify: cardiac tamponade, massive PE (RV dilation), severe hypovolemia, pneumothorax, and presence/absence of cardiac activity. A trained operator should perform POCUS during the pulse check window only.

Special Resuscitation Situations
🤰 Cardiac Arrest in Pregnancy

Manual uterine displacement (push uterus to the left) to relieve aortocaval compression. Perform perimortem cesarean section (PMCS) at or after 20 weeks gestation if no ROSC within 4–5 min of maternal arrest. PMCS improves maternal and fetal outcomes. Standard defibrillation energy is safe for fetus.

❄️ Hypothermic Cardiac Arrest

No patient is dead until warm and dead. In profound hypothermia (< 30°C): limit initial shocks to 3 before warming; withhold epinephrine if temp < 30°C; ECMO (ECPR) is preferred rewarming method. Standard ACLS resumes when core temp > 30°C. Max survival reported in hypothermic arrest even after hours of CPR.

⚡ Electrical Injury / Lightning

Remove patient from danger first. Lightning: multiple victims — treat apparently dead first (inverse triage). Spinal precautions if indicated. Standard ACLS. High risk of prolonged apnea; continue CPR even with apparent signs of life loss.

🏊 Drowning / Near-Drowning

Hypoxia is primary cause — prioritize ventilation (5 rescue breaths before CPR, if possible). Hypothermia is common and neuroprotective. Remove from water; begin CPR immediately. Spinal injury rare unless diving. Do not perform abdominal thrusts to remove water.

💉 Opioid-Associated Cardiac Arrest

Naloxone (0.4–2 mg IV/IM/IN) for respiratory arrest or severe respiratory depression. For frank cardiac arrest, standard ACLS takes priority over naloxone — CPR is the critical intervention. Repeat naloxone every 2–3 min as needed. Caution: may precipitate acute withdrawal.

🩸 TCA Overdose

Wide QRS (> 100 ms), tall R in aVR. Treatment: Sodium Bicarbonate 1–2 mEq/kg IV bolus (alkalinize serum; target pH 7.45–7.55). Avoid class IA/IC antiarrhythmics, amiodarone (QT prolongation). Lipid emulsion therapy as rescue. Consider VA-ECMO.

🏃 Commotio Cordis

Blunt precordial trauma (usually projectile or fist) during the vulnerable T-wave period → VF. Survival depends on immediate CPR and defibrillation. Often in young athletes. Standard ACLS. Protective chest guards may reduce risk.

🏥 ECPR (Extracorporeal CPR)

VA-ECMO deployed during refractory cardiac arrest. Indications: witnessed arrest, shockable rhythm, < 60 min CPR, reversible etiology (PE, hypothermia, poisoning). Requires cardiac surgery team. Time-critical: cannulation < 60 min from arrest improves outcomes significantly.

📌 Remember: ACLS Is a Framework, Not a Recipe

Clinical judgment, team communication, and high-quality CPR are more important than any single drug or protocol. Identify the cause. Treat the patient, not the monitor. Debrief after every resuscitation — continuous improvement saves lives.
