📘 Sepsis Guidelines 2026

Surviving Sepsis Campaign — International Guidelines for Management of Sepsis and Septic Shock

📊 Guidelines at a Glance

The Surviving Sepsis Campaign (SSC) is a joint initiative of the Society of Critical Care Medicine (SCCM) and the European Society of Intensive Care Medicine (ESICM). Since the first edition in 2004, the guidelines have been updated periodically to incorporate the latest evidence. The 2026 update represents the most comprehensive revision to date, with 129 statements — including 46 entirely new recommendations — and a stronger focus on patient‑centered outcomes, antimicrobial stewardship, and post‑discharge continuity of care.

🆕 Surviving Sepsis Campaign 2026

Published: 23 March 2026 | Citation: Prescott H, Antonelli M, Alhazzani W, et al. Crit Care Med. 2026 Mar. doi:10.1097/CCM.0000000000007075

The 2026 guidelines update the 2021 version and contain 129 statements, of which 46 are new. The panel comprised 69 members from 23 countries; 38% had practiced in low‑ or middle‑income countries.

✨ Major New Recommendations (2026)

🔍 Screening & Early Identification ▼

• Performance improvement programs: Strong recommendation (“recommend”) for hospitals to use sepsis screening, standard operating procedures, and quality improvement strategies.

• “Code sepsis” / “sepsis huddle”: Suggest using a multidisciplinary bedside huddle protocol to expedite diagnosis and treatment.

• Pre‑hospital screening: Suggest a standard sepsis screening tool for adults en route to hospital by ambulance or flight.

• In‑hospital screening tools: Recommend NEWS, NEW2, MEWS, or SIRS over qSOFA as a single screening tool (moderate certainty).

💊 Antimicrobial Therapy ▼

• Timing: For septic shock, administer antimicrobials immediately, ideally within 1 h. For sepsis without shock, within 1 h (probable/definite) or within 3 h (possible).

• Pre‑hospital antibiotics: Suggest administration in ambulance/flight if time to hospital >60 min for septic shock patients.

• Procalcitonin: Suggest against using procalcitonin plus clinical evaluation to decide antibiotic initiation (use clinical evaluation alone).

• MDR risk: Tailor empiric coverage based on individual MDR risk factors.

• Prolonged beta‑lactam infusion: Recommend prolonged infusion over bolus administration (moderate certainty).

• De‑escalation: Recommend de‑escalation when microbiology results are available; suggest de‑escalation even when cultures are negative.

💧 Hemodynamic Resuscitation ▼

• Initial fluid: Suggest ≥30 mL/kg IV crystalloid in the first 3 h for hypoperfusion/septic shock (low certainty).

• Balanced crystalloids: Suggest balanced solutions over 0.9% saline (moderate certainty).

• Vasopressor initiation: Suggest starting vasopressors peripherally rather than delaying for central access.

• MAP target: Recommend initial MAP 65 mm Hg (moderate certainty); for adults ≥65 years, suggest MAP 60‑65 mm Hg.

• Active fluid removal: After the acute resuscitation phase, suggest active fluid removal (diuretics/ultrafiltration).

🫁 Respiratory Support ▼

• HFNC: Suggest high‑flow nasal cannula as the initial device for sepsis‑associated acute hypoxemic respiratory failure.

• Awake proning: Suggest a trial of awake proning in non‑intubated patients.

• Tidal volume: For sepsis‑associated hypoxemic respiratory failure without ARDS, suggest 6‑8 mL/kg IBW.

• ARDS ventilation: Recommend low tidal volume (6 mL/kg) and plateau pressure ≤30 cm H₂O.

🧪 Adjunctive Therapies ▼

• Corticosteroids: Suggest IV corticosteroids for adults with septic shock.

• Against antipyretics: Suggest against routine antipyretic therapy to improve outcomes (may be used for symptom relief).

• Against vitamin C, immunoglobulins, blood purification: Suggest against these therapies.

• Selective digestive decontamination (SDD): Suggest SDD in units with low antimicrobial resistance prevalence.

🏡 Transitions of Care ▼

• Goals of care: Suggest addressing goals of care early (within 72 h).

• Medication reconciliation: Suggest pharmacist‑based comprehensive medication reconciliation at transitions of care.

• Discharge planning: Provide written and verbal information about sepsis, treatments, and common impairments after sepsis.

• Post‑discharge follow‑up: Hospital discharge plans should include follow‑up for new impairments and long‑term sequelae.

📌 Note: The 2026 guidelines are the first to explicitly recommend active fluid removal after the acute resuscitation phase and to provide a separate, lower MAP target for older adults (60‑65 mm Hg).

🧬 Sepsis‑3 Definitions & qSOFA

Published: February 2016 | Citation: Singer M, Deutschman CS, Seymour CW, et al. The Third International Consensus Definitions for Sepsis and Septic Shock (Sepsis‑3). JAMA. 2016;315(8):801‑810.

  • Sepsis is now defined as “life‑threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection.”
  • Septic shock is defined as hypotension requiring vasopressors to maintain MAP ≥65 mm Hg and serum lactate >2 mmol/L despite adequate volume resuscitation (associated with >40% in‑hospital mortality).
  • The term “severe sepsis” has been eliminated.
  • SIRS criteria are no longer part of the sepsis definition; instead, the SOFA score (increase ≥2 points) is used to identify organ dysfunction.

🩺 qSOFA (quick Sequential Organ Failure Assessment)

CriterionThreshold
Respiratory rate≥22 breaths/min
Systolic blood pressure≤100 mm Hg
Altered mental statusGlasgow Coma Scale <15 (or any acute change)
⚠️ Important: qSOFA is not a screening tool for sepsis. The 2021 and 2026 guidelines recommend against using qSOFA as a single screening tool. Use NEWS, MEWS, or SIRS criteria instead.
