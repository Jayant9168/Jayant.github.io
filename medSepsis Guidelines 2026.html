Surviving Sepsis Campaign 2026

Comprehensive Adult Clinical Reference

The 2026 adult Surviving Sepsis Campaign (SSC) guideline was published on March 23, 2026, updating the 2021 edition. It contains 129 statements (46 new), developed by 69 experts from 23 countries, and endorsed by 24 professional societies. The guideline sharpens clinical decision-making across hospital, prehospital, and post-hospital settings. [web:2][page:2]

Published: March 23, 2026 129 total statements 46 new recommendations 69 experts, 23 countries Endorsed by 24 societies Replaces 2021 adult guideline

Major new and revised recommendations in 2026

2026 Updates
Prehospital care: The 2026 guideline adds prehospital recommendations for the first time in this detail, including a standard screening tool during ambulance or flight transport, and prehospital antibiotics in selected septic shock cases when hospital evaluation will be delayed over 60 minutes. [web:2]
  • Screening: Use NEWS, NEWS2, MEWS, or SIRS over qSOFA as a single screening tool in hospitalized adults. qSOFA alone is not recommended as the sole screen. [page:2]
  • Code Sepsis / Sepsis Huddle: New conditional recommendation for “code sepsis” or “sepsis huddle” protocols to expedite recognition and treatment. [page:2]
  • Performance improvement: Recommends sepsis quality improvement programs with feedback, protocolization, and regular audit. [page:2]
  • Fluid type: Suggests balanced crystalloids over 0.9% saline for initial resuscitation. [page:2]
  • Albumin: Suggests crystalloids alone over crystalloids plus albumin for fluid resuscitation; albumin may be considered after large crystalloid volumes or in patients with cirrhosis. This is a shift from 2021. [page:2]
  • Post-30 mL/kg fluid strategy: Suggests either liberal or restrictive further fluid strategy based on patient and system factors after initial resuscitation. [page:2]
  • Active fluid removal: New recommendation to suggest active fluid removal after the acute resuscitation phase in septic shock. [page:2]
  • Peripheral vasopressors: Suggests starting vasopressors peripherally rather than delaying until central venous access is secured. [page:2]
  • MAP targets: Initial MAP target remains 65 mmHg; for adults aged 65 years or older, suggests a target of 60–65 mmHg. [page:2]
  • Anaerobic coverage: New guidance to avoid empiric anaerobic coverage when no risk factors exist, and to use it when risk factors are present. [page:2]
  • Beta-lactam infusion: Upgraded to a recommendation (stronger than 2021) for prolonged infusion after an initial loading dose. [page:2]
  • Lactate: Suggests serial lactate measurements to guide resuscitation, but emphasizes fluids should be individualized and not continued solely to normalize lactate. [page:2]
  • Post-discharge: Expanded framework for discharge planning, medication reconciliation, patient/family education, physical rehabilitation, and mental health support. [page:2]

2026 Resuscitation & hemodynamic bundle

Core care
  • Recognize early: Use NEWS/NEWS2/MEWS/SIRS in hospitalized adults. Consider “code sepsis” or “sepsis huddle” activation.
  • Cultures: Obtain blood cultures (and other relevant cultures) before antibiotics if this does not substantially delay antibiotic administration.
  • Antibiotics: Administer immediate, effective IV antibiotics ideally within 1 hour for septic shock and for probable/definite sepsis without shock. If only possible sepsis without shock, rapid workup and antibiotics within 3 hours if concern persists.
  • Fluids: Give at least 30 mL/kg IV crystalloid for septic hypoperfusion or septic shock within the first 3 hours. Use balanced crystalloids over 0.9% saline. Reassess frequently and individualize.
  • Vasopressors: Start norepinephrine first-line to maintain MAP ≥65 mmHg (or 60–65 mmHg if age ≥65). Peripheral initiation is acceptable to avoid delay.
  • Dynamic assessment: Use dynamic measures to assess fluid responsiveness rather than static measures alone.
  • Lactate: Measure serial lactate to guide resuscitation, but do not continue fluids solely to normalize lactate.
  • Active fluid removal: Consider after acute resuscitation in septic shock.

2026 Infection management details

Antimicrobial
  • Empiric therapy: Use broad-spectrum agents covering all likely pathogens; de-escalate once susceptibilities known.
  • Anaerobes: Avoid empiric anaerobic coverage when no risk factors for anaerobic infection are present. Use anaerobic coverage when risk factors exist.
  • Beta-lactams: Recommend prolonged infusion after an initial loading dose to optimize pharmacokinetics.
  • Combination therapy: For septic shock, combination empiric therapy may be considered depending on local resistance patterns and patient risk factors.
  • Duration: Shortest effective duration; typically 7–10 days if clinically appropriate. Longer courses for slow response, undrainable foci, or immunologic deficiencies.
  • Source control: Pursue as soon as practical after resuscitation is initiated.
  • Biomarkers: Procalcitonin may be used to support shortening antibiotic duration, but should not be used alone to decide initiation.

Adjunctive therapies

Supportive
  • Corticosteroids: IV corticosteroids for septic shock with ongoing vasopressor requirement.
  • Glucose: Target <180 mg/dL using a protocolized approach.
  • Transfusion: Transfuse RBCs only when hemoglobin <7 g/dL (unless extenuating circumstances).
  • Mechanical ventilation: Lung-protective strategy if ARDS develops.
  • Sedation: Minimize continuous sedation; use protocolized, targeted light sedation.
  • VTE prophylaxis: Pharmacologic prophylaxis unless contraindicated.
  • Stress ulcer: Use GI bleeding prophylaxis for patients with GI bleeding risk factors.
  • Nutrition: Enteral nutrition preferred over parenteral when feasible.

Hemodynamic targets

Targets
MAP: 65 mmHg for most adults.
Older adults (≥65): 60–65 mmHg suggested.
  • Dynamic measures (e.g., passive leg raise, stroke volume variation) preferred over static measures for fluid responsiveness.
  • Peripheral vasopressors acceptable to avoid treatment delays.
  • Add vasopressin if norepinephrine inadequate; epinephrine as alternative.
  • Dobutamine for myocardial dysfunction or ongoing hypoperfusion despite adequate volume and MAP.

Post-hospital & survivorship

Expanded
The 2026 guideline significantly expands post-discharge recommendations, reflecting growing recognition of post-sepsis morbidity.
  • Structured discharge planning with medication reconciliation.
  • Patient and family education on sepsis, warning signs, and when to seek care.
  • Physical rehabilitation and functional assessment.
  • Mental health screening and support (anxiety, depression, PTSD).
  • Follow-up appointments with primary care and relevant specialists.
  • Consideration of post-ICU clinics or survivorship programs.

Historical timeline of SSC adult guidelines

Evolution
2004
First SSC guideline. Introduced the concept of bundled care for severe sepsis and septic shock. Focus on early recognition, antibiotics, fluids, and source control. [web:10]
2008
Second major update. Refined bundles and incorporated new evidence on corticosteroids, glucose control, and recombinant human activated protein C. [web:10]
2012 / 2013
2012 SSC guideline. Revision of 2008 guidance accepted in 2012 and published in 2013. Emphasized protocolized care, sepsis bundles, and performance improvement. [web:10]
2016
Fourth major era. Aligned with Sepsis-3 definitions (published 2016 by SCCM/ESICM), shifting from SIRS-based to organ dysfunction-based recognition. qSOFA introduced as bedside prompt. [web:7]
2021
2021 update. Strengthened recommendations on balanced crystalloids, peripheral vasopressors, and expanded post-ICU/survivorship planning. 90 statements with updated evidence. [page:1]
2026
Current guideline. 129 statements, 46 new. Adds prehospital care, code sepsis protocols, refined MAP targets for older adults, active fluid removal, anaerobic stewardship, and broader post-discharge care. [web:2][page:2]

How definitions changed over time

Sepsis-3
Pre-2016: Sepsis was defined as infection + SIRS criteria. Severe sepsis = sepsis + organ dysfunction. Septic shock = sepsis + hypotension despite fluids.
Sepsis-3 (2016): Sepsis is life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection. Septic shock = sepsis + persisting hypotension requiring vasopressors to maintain MAP ≥65 mmHg + serum lactate >2 mmol/L despite adequate volume resuscitation.
  • The 2016, 2021, and 2026 SSC guidelines all use Sepsis-3 definitions.
  • qSOFA (≥2 of: altered mentation, SBP ≤100 mmHg, RR ≥22) was introduced in 2016 as a bedside prompt for organ dysfunction, not as a diagnostic test.
  • The 2021 and 2026 guidelines explicitly recommend against using qSOFA as the sole screening tool, favoring NEWS/NEWS2/MEWS/SIRS for hospitalized adults.
