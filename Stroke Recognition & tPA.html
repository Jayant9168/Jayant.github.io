BE-FAST Stroke Recognition

BE-FAST is the globally recommended stroke recognition tool, endorsed by the World Stroke Organization (WSO) and AHA/ASA. It captures posterior circulation strokes that FAST misses (≈14% of strokes). Posterior strokes are frequently misdiagnosed as vertigo, gastritis, or intoxication worldwide.

B
Balance Sudden loss of balance, coordination, or unexplained falls — hallmark of posterior circulation stroke
E
Eyes Double vision, blurry vision, or vision loss in one/both eyes — often dismissed as fatigue or eye strain
F
Face Drooping or numbness on one side. Ask patient to smile or show teeth.
A
Arm Weakness or numbness in one arm/leg. Ask to raise both arms for 10 seconds.
S
Speech Slurred, garbled, or unable to speak. Ask to name common objects or repeat a phrase.
T
Time Call emergency services immediately! Note exact time of symptom onset. Every minute = 2 million neurons lost.
⚠️ Global Reality: Prehospital delay is the #1 barrier to thrombolysis worldwide. Up to 70% of patients in LMICs first visit a local practitioner instead of emergency services. Community education using local-language BE-FAST campaigns is critical.

🔴 Top 10 Modifiable Risk Factors (GBD 2021)

  • High blood pressure — #1 risk factor globally; accounts for ~48% of stroke burden
  • Air pollution — ambient PM2.5; major contributor in South Asia, East Asia, sub-Saharan Africa
  • Smoking / tobacco use — cigarettes, bidis, water-pipe, smokeless tobacco
  • High LDL cholesterol — diet-driven; rising in LMICs with nutrition transition
  • High sodium intake — processed foods, preserved foods, soy sauce
  • High fasting plasma glucose / diabetes — fastest-growing risk factor globally
  • Kidney dysfunction — chronic kidney disease increases stroke risk 2-3x
  • Excess body weight / obesity — BMI-driven; epidemic in HICs and urban LMICs
  • Physical inactivity — sedentary lifestyles; urbanization driver
  • Harmful alcohol use — binge drinking increases ICH risk significantly

NIHSS Scoring System

The National Institutes of Health Stroke Scale (NIHSS) is the global gold standard for quantifying stroke severity. It is a 15-item neurological examination (score 0–42) used universally in stroke trials, registries, and clinical practice. Higher scores correlate with worse outcomes and guide tPA/EVT eligibility.

ScoreSeverityClinical Implication
0–4Minor StrokeGood functional outcome likely; tPA may be deferred if rapidly improving
5–15Moderate StroketPA candidate; monitor closely; ~50% achieve mRS 0–2 at 90 days
16–20Moderate-SevereHigh disability risk; consider EVT + tPA; ICU monitoring
21–25Severe StrokeCritical; tPA contraindicated if >25 for 3–4.5h window (ECASS-III)
>25Very Severe / ComaPoor prognosis; score 42 = deep coma; consider palliation
📋 NIHSS 15 Items: 1a. LOC, 1b. LOC Questions, 1c. LOC Commands, 2. Best Gaze, 3. Visual Fields, 4. Facial Palsy, 5a. Motor Arm (L), 5b. Motor Arm (R), 6a. Motor Leg (L), 6b. Motor Leg (R), 7. Limb Ataxia, 8. Sensory, 9. Best Language, 10. Dysarthria, 11. Extinction & Inattention.

Door-to-Needle Time Targets (Global)

AHA/ASA Target: Stroke and WSO guidelines set the global standard. Time is brain: approximately 2 million neurons are lost per minute of ischemia. Every 15-minute delay in tPA administration reduces the odds of a good outcome by 4–5%.

0 min — Hospital Arrival
🚑 ED Triage / Stroke Code Activation
Activate stroke team immediately. Pre-hospital notification (if available) saves 15–30 minutes. Use standardized handoff protocols.
10 min
🩺 Physician Assessment + NIHSS
Rapid neuro exam, vitals, capillary glucose. Document NIHSS. Rule out stroke mimics (hypoglycemia, seizure, migraine).
25 min
🧠 Brain Imaging (NCCT Head)
Non-contrast CT to exclude hemorrhage. Target: imaging within 25 min. CTA if large vessel occlusion suspected. Tele-radiology for remote centers.
45 min
💉 tPA Bolus (Secondary Goal)
Target: 50% of patients treated within 45 min. 10% IV bolus over 1 minute. BP must be <185/110 mmHg before administration.
60 min
💉 tPA Infusion Complete (Primary Goal)
Target: 75% of patients within 60 min. 90% infusion over 60 minutes. Mortality 8.6% vs 10.4% if treated ≤60 min vs >60 min.
60 min
Primary Door-to-Needle Target (AHA/ASA / WSO)
Every 15 min delay = 4-5% ↓ odds of good outcome · 2M neurons lost/min

tPA Contraindications (AHA/ASA / WSO)

Per AHA/ASA 2024–2026 Guidelines and WSO recommendations. IV alteplase (0.9 mg/kg, max 90 mg) is the standard of care within 3 hours; extended to 3–4.5 hours with additional exclusions per ECASS-III.

Absolute Do NOT Give tPA

  • ✕ Active intracranial hemorrhage on CT
  • ✕ Structural intracranial disease (AVM, tumor, aneurysm)
  • ✕ Ischemic stroke within past 3 months
  • ✕ Active bleeding or bleeding diathesis
  • ✕ Platelet count <100,000/mm³
  • ✕ Recent brain/spinal surgery (≤3 months)
  • ✕ Recent head trauma with fracture or brain injury
  • ✕ Symptoms suggesting subarachnoid hemorrhage
  • ✕ Glucose <50 mg/dL (2.8 mmol/L) — correct first
  • ✕ INR >1.7 on warfarin (or elevated PT/aPTT)
  • ✕ Direct thrombin inhibitors or Factor Xa inhibitors (unless stopped >48h and normal labs)
  • ✕ LMWH at treatment doses within previous 24 hours

Relative Weigh Risk vs Benefit

  • ! SBP >180 or DBP >110 mmHg (must lower to <185/110 before tPA)
  • ! Age >80 years (exclusion for 3–4.5h window per ECASS-III)
  • ! NIHSS score >25 (exclusion for 3–4.5h window)
  • ! History of both diabetes AND prior stroke (3–4.5h exclusion)
  • ! Seizure at onset with postictal deficit
  • ! Major surgery in preceding 3 months
  • ! Recent MI, GI/GU bleeding, or arterial puncture at non-compressible site
  • ! Pregnancy (limited data; consider if severe deficit)
  • ! Imaging evidence of ischemic injury >1/3 MCA territory
  • ! Early ischemic changes on CT (subtle hypodensity may be acceptable)

tPA (Alteplase) Protocol — Global Standard

IV rtPA (Alteplase) is the first evidence-based treatment for acute ischemic stroke, approved by FDA (1996), EMA, and regulatory bodies worldwide. Tenecteplase (TNK) is increasingly adopted as a single-bolus alternative with comparable efficacy and easier administration.

0.9
mg/kg (max 90 mg)
Alteplase standard dose
4.5h
Maximum time window
From known symptom onset

📋 Step-by-Step Administration

  • Step 1: Calculate total dose = 0.9 mg/kg (round to nearest mg, maximum 90 mg)
  • Step 2: Administer 10% as IV bolus over 1 minute
  • Step 3: Infuse remaining 90% over 60 minutes via infusion pump
  • Step 4: No antiplatelet or anticoagulant for 24 hours post-tPA
  • Step 5: BP checks every 15 min × 2h, then every 30 min × 6h, then hourly × 16h
  • Step 6: NIHSS assessment at 2h and 24h post-tPA
  • Step 7: Repeat CT/MRI at 24h before starting antithrombotics
  • Step 8: Continuous monitoring for angioedema, bleeding, neurological deterioration
⚠️ BP Management During/After tPA:
• Before tPA: BP must be <185/110 mmHg (labetalol 10-20 mg IV or nicardipine infusion)
• During & 24h after: Maintain BP <180/105 mmHg
• If SBP >220 or DBP >120: Aggressive management with IV agents required

⚠️ Complications to Monitor

  • 🩸 Symptomatic ICH (sICH) — 6-7% risk. Stop infusion immediately, emergency CT, neurosurgery consult, reverse coagulopathy.
  • 🩸 Systemic bleeding — Apply pressure, check coagulation profile, transfuse blood products if needed.
  • 🧠 Angioedema — 1-5% risk (often orolingual). Airway management, IV steroids, antihistamines. Intubate if airway compromise.
  • 💔 Reperfusion injury / cerebral edema — Mannitol 20% 1g/kg or hypertonic saline if herniation signs.
  • 🫁 Hypotension / arrhythmia — Monitor cardiac rhythm for 24h (screen for AF).

tPA binds fibrin in the clot → converts plasminogen to plasmin → fibrinolysis → vessel recanalization

Tenecteplase (TNK) — Global Alternative:
• 0.25 mg/kg single IV bolus (max 25 mg) — no infusion pump needed
• Faster administration, less dosing error, ideal for prehospital/remote settings
• Comparable efficacy to alteplase in EXTEND-IA TNK, AcT, and NOR-TEST trials
• Approved in Australia, Canada, parts of Europe; expanding globally
• Check local regulatory approval and institutional protocol

Neuro Emergencies & Stroke Mimics

Stroke mimics account for 15–30% of suspected stroke presentations globally. Misdiagnosis leads to unnecessary tPA exposure or missed treatment windows. Systematic differentiation is essential before thrombolysis.

🔴 Status Epilepticus / Todd's Paralysis

Postictal Todd's paralysis can perfectly mimic stroke. Rule: If seizure at onset → tPA contraindicated unless deficit persists >30 min post-seizure. Check EEG if available.

🔴 Hypoglycemia

Glucose <50 mg/dL (2.8 mmol/L) causes focal neuro deficits indistinguishable from stroke. Always check capillary glucose first. Give IV dextrose if low — if symptoms resolve, it is NOT stroke.

🔴 Hypertensive Encephalopathy / PRES

SBP >220 with altered mental status, seizures, visual loss. Lower BP gradually (15-20% in 1st hour). Do NOT give tPA. MRI shows posterior white matter hyperintensities.

🔴 Subarachnoid Hemorrhage (SAH)

"Thunderclap" headache, nuchal rigidity, vomiting. CT shows blood in sulci/cisterns. tPA is ABSOLUTE contraindication. Needs neurosurgery / endovascular coiling.

🔴 Meningitis / Encephalitis

Fever + neck stiffness + altered sensorium + focal deficits. LP for CSF analysis. Do NOT give tPA. Start empiric antibiotics/antivirals immediately.

🔴 Migraine with Aura (Hemiplegic)

Can mimic stroke, especially in young females. If atypical or first episode → full stroke workup before excluding tPA. History of similar episodes helps.

🔴 Functional Neurological Disorder (Conversion Disorder)

Inconsistent examination, non-anatomical deficits, give-way weakness, la belle indifference. Do NOT give tPA. Psychiatric referral.

🔴 Brain Tumor / Abscess

Subacute onset, progressive deficits, papilledema. CT/MRI with contrast. Steroids for edema. Neurosurgery referral.

Global Stroke Care Systems

Organized stroke care systems dramatically improve outcomes. The World Stroke Organization advocates for tiered networks, telemedicine, and Mobile Stroke Units to bridge the urban-rural and HIC-LMIC gap.

🏛️ Stroke Center Classification (Global)

  • Primary Stroke Center (PSC): 24/7 CT, tPA capability, basic stroke unit, stroke protocols. District/regional hospitals.
  • Comprehensive Stroke Center (CSC): PSC + 24/7 neurosurgery, EVT (thrombectomy), neuro-ICU, neuro-intervention. Tertiary/quaternary centers.
  • Acute Stroke-Ready Hospital (ASRH): Can stabilize, give tPA, then transfer to CSC. Community/rural hospitals.
  • Telestroke Hub-and-Spoke Model: CSC (hub) provides real-time video consultation to remote hospitals (spokes) for tPA decision-making, image interpretation, and transfer coordination.
🚑 Mobile Stroke Units (MSU) — Global Adoption:
• Germany — First MSU (2010); proven to reduce treatment time by 30–60 min
• USA — Multiple cities (Houston, Cleveland, New York); improved tPA rates
• Australia — Rural MSU networks for vast remote areas
• Brazil — SAMU-MSU in São Paulo for urban dense populations
• China — Stroke 120 campaign + MSU in major cities
• India — ICMR-funded MSU in Assam; 24h → 2h treatment time; deaths reduced 1/3
• Key finding: MSUs consistently improve door-to-needle times and functional outcomes

📊 Global Barriers to Stroke Care

  • Prehospital delay: #1 barrier globally. 70% of LMIC patients visit local practitioners first.
  • Low thrombolysis rates: 10-15% in HICs vs <1% in many LMICs.
  • Infrastructure gaps: No 24/7 CT in many district hospitals; no stroke physicians.
  • Financial barriers: tPA cost (~$3,000–8,000) prohibitive without universal health coverage.
  • Workforce shortage: Neurologist density 0.03/100,000 in Africa vs 4/100,000 in Europe.
  • Awareness: Low public knowledge of stroke symptoms in all regions; language barriers for FAST/BE-FAST.
  • Climate change: Rising temperatures and air pollution increasing stroke incidence globally (ISC 2026).
📋 WHO Best Buys for Stroke Prevention:
The WHO recommends cost-effective interventions: tobacco taxation, salt reduction policies, trans-fat elimination, hypertension screening & treatment, diabetes management, and atrial fibrillation detection & anticoagulation. These "best buys" can prevent 80% of cardiovascular events including stroke.
