Abstract

Objectives We aimed to evaluate the clinical profile, imaging characteristics, management strategies, and independent prognostic determinants of outcome in patients presenting with subdural haematoma (SDH) to a tertiary emergency department in South India.

Design Prospective observational cohort with retrospective record review.

Setting Single-centre study at JIPMER (Jawaharlal Institute of Postgraduate Medical Education and Research), a national tertiary emergency referral centre in Puducherry, Southern India, from January 2023 to December 2024.

Participants 270 adult patients with CT- and/or MRI-confirmed SDH. Inclusion criteria were age ≥18 years, CT/MRI-confirmed SDH, and presentation during the study period. Exclusion criteria were isolated spinal SDH, isolated traumatic epidural haematoma, and incomplete records.

Interventions Surgical management (burr-hole drainage, craniotomy, decompressive craniectomy, or middle meningeal artery embolisation) or conservative management with imaging surveillance.

Main outcome measures Primary outcome: Glasgow Outcome Scale (GOS) at 30 days, dichotomised as favourable (GOS 4–5) versus unfavourable (GOS 1–3). Secondary outcomes: 30-day mortality, surgical complications, and chronic SDH recurrence. Independent predictors were identified using multivariate binary logistic regression.

Results Of 270 patients (mean age 48.6±18.4 years; 78.1% male), acute SDH was the most frequent subtype (49.6%), followed by chronic SDH (30.4%). Road traffic crashes accounted for 43.7% of cases. Technical success was achieved in all surgical patients; favourable 30-day GOS (4–5) was attained in 59.3%. The 30-day mortality was 20.0%. Among 82 patients with chronic SDH, 17 (20.9%) developed recurrence at 90 days; those receiving adjunct MMA embolisation (n=18) had lower recurrence compared with burr-hole drainage alone (8.3% vs 25.0%; p=0.04). On multivariate analysis, independent predictors of unfavourable outcome were GCS ≤8 at admission (AOR 8.4; 95% CI 4.2 to 16.8; p<0.001), bilateral fixed dilated pupils (AOR 12.1; 95% CI 5.6 to 26.2; p<0.001), midline shift >10 mm (AOR 6.3; 95% CI 3.1 to 12.8; p<0.001), basal cistern obliteration (AOR 9.8; 95% CI 4.4 to 21.8; p<0.001), and time from injury to surgery exceeding 4 hours in acute SDH (AOR 3.6; 95% CI 1.8 to 7.2; p=0.003).

Conclusions In our cohort, GCS at admission and bilateral fixed pupils were the dominant independent predictors of poor outcome, while basal cistern obliteration appeared to be the strongest imaging predictor of mortality. A streamlined 4-hour surgical pathway and adjunctive MMA embolisation for chronic SDH may improve outcomes, but these findings require confirmation in larger multicentre or randomised studies.

⇒ GCS at admission, pupillary status, and CT imaging parameters are established prognostic markers in SDH, but most evidence comes from Western neurosurgical centres; region-specific prospective data from South Asian emergency departments remain limited.
What this study adds
⇒ Road traffic crashes dominate SDH aetiology in this South Indian cohort (43.7%); bilateral fixed pupils (OR 12.1) and basal cistern obliteration (OR 9.8) were the strongest independent predictors of 30-day unfavourable outcome.
⇒ Adjunct MMA embolisation was associated with lower CSDH recurrence compared with burr-hole drainage alone (8.3% vs 25.0%; p=0.04), supporting its emerging role in chronic SDH management.

⇒ Emergency departments in South Asia could consider implementing a 4-hour craniotomy pathway as a quality metric, and randomised trials should evaluate MMA embolisation protocols for CSDH in LMIC settings.
Introduction

Subdural haematoma (SDH) — the collection of blood between the dura and arachnoid membranes — represents a spectrum from rapidly fatal acute haemorrhage to insidiously progressive chronic collections in the elderly.1,4 Acute SDH may progress to transtentorial herniation within hours, whereas chronic SDH (CSDH) often presents with non-specific symptoms — headache, cognitive decline, unsteady gait — that are easily attributed to ageing.5 The global epidemiology of SDH is shifting: an ageing population and widespread anticoagulant use are driving a rise in CSDH in high-income countries,14 while in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs) such as India, acute SDH following road traffic crashes — particularly among young, unprotected two-wheeler riders — disproportionately drives emergency presentations. Non-contrast CT remains the gold standard for emergency diagnosis, with MRI providing additional value in subacute and chronic phases.12,13 The landmark study by Seelig et al demonstrated that craniotomy within 4 hours in comatose patients reduced mortality from 90% to 30%,1 a finding that reshaped SDH management globally.

Despite this evidence base, region-specific outcome data from South Asian emergency departments are sparse. Most prognostic series come from high-income neurosurgical centres, and extrapolation to LMIC settings — where pre-hospital delays, resource constraints, and a different aetiological mix are the norm — may not hold. We identified a clear knowledge gap regarding the relative weight of clinical, radiological, and management-related predictors in South Indian patients managed under real-world emergency conditions.

The objective of this study was to describe the clinical and radiological profile of patients with SDH presenting to a South Indian tertiary emergency department and to identify independent predictors of 30-day outcome.

Methods
Study design and setting

We conducted this prospective observational cohort study with retrospective record review at a single tertiary emergency centre (JIPMER, Puducherry, South India) between January 2023 and December 2024. Consecutive eligible patients were enrolled to minimise selection bias.

Inclusion and exclusion criteria

Inclusion criteria: age ≥18 years; CT and/or MRI-confirmed SDH; presentation to our emergency department during the study period.

Exclusion criteria: isolated spinal SDH; isolated traumatic epidural haematoma without SDH component; incomplete clinical or imaging records precluding outcome assessment.

Data collection and outcomes

Data were collected by trained emergency medicine residents using a pre-structured proforma, supplemented by retrospective review of inpatient records and imaging archives. The Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) was assessed at triage by the attending emergency physician.3 Pupillary status was documented by penlight examination as reactive, sluggishly reactive, or fixed and dilated (unilateral or bilateral). Missing data were handled by listwise deletion; no imputation was performed.

The primary outcome was the Glasgow Outcome Scale (GOS) at 30 days, dichotomised as favourable (GOS 4–5) versus unfavourable (GOS 1–3). Secondary outcomes were 30-day mortality, surgical complications (hospital-acquired pneumonia, post-operative seizures, DVT/PE), and CSDH recurrence (symptomatic re-expansion confirmed on CT requiring re-intervention within 90 days). Follow-up was through outpatient department review and structured telephone interviews; 12 patients (4.4%) were lost to 30-day follow-up and were excluded from the outcome analysis.

Demographic data (age, sex, domicile), clinical parameters (haemodynamic status, comorbidities, anticoagulant use, coagulation profile), radiological findings (SDH subtype, laterality, haematoma thickness, midline shift, volume by ABC/2 method, CT density, basal cistern status, associated injuries, MRI sequences), and management details (procedure type, time from injury to surgery) were extracted.

Statistical analysis

Data were analysed using IBM SPSS V.26.0. We report means (SD) or medians (IQR) for continuous variables and frequencies (%) for categorical variables. Variables with p<0.10 on univariate analysis or strong a priori clinical relevance were entered into a multivariable binary logistic regression model. Results are reported as adjusted odds ratios (AOR) with 95% confidence intervals. Model fit was assessed using the Hosmer–Lemeshow test and Nagelkerke pseudo-R²; variance inflation factors (VIFs) were examined for multicollinearity. Statistical significance was set at p<0.05.

Patient and public involvement

Patients or the public were not involved in the design, conduct, or dissemination of this study. Written informed consent was obtained from all participants or their legally authorised representatives. The study was approved by the JIPMER Institute Ethics Committee.

Results

During the 24-month study period, 282 patients with SDH were screened; 12 were excluded (incomplete records, n=8; isolated epidural haematoma, n=4), leaving 270 for analysis (Table 1). The mean age was 48.6±18.4 years, with the largest group aged 41–60 years (35.6%). Males predominated (78.1%). Most patients (83.7%) were haemodynamically stable at presentation. Coagulopathy (PT/INR >1.5) was present in 7.0%. Hypertension was reported in 41.5% and diabetes in 28.9%.

Acute SDH was the most common subtype (49.6%), followed by chronic (30.4%), subacute (14.1%), and acute-on-chronic (5.9%). Road traffic crashes accounted for 43.7% of cases, ground-level falls for 27.4%, falls from height for 8.9%, and assault for 6.7%. No recalled trauma was documented in 8.1%. Bilateral SDH was seen in 12.6%.

Surgical intervention was undertaken in 195 patients (72.2%): burr-hole drainage was the most common procedure (31.9%), followed by emergency craniotomy (21.5%), decompressive craniectomy (5.2%), and MMA embolisation (6.7%, n=18, used as adjunct or primary treatment for CSDH). Conservative management was pursued in 75 patients (27.8%).

Figure 3 summarises the key outcomes. Technical success was achieved in all 195 surgical patients (100%). Favourable 30-day outcome (GOS 4–5) was attained in 160 patients (59.3%). Immediate complications occurred in 44 patients (16.3%), most commonly hospital-acquired pneumonia and post-operative seizures. The 30-day mortality was 20.0% (n=54). Among the 82 patients with chronic SDH, 3-month survival was 100%, and recurrence requiring re-intervention occurred in 17 (20.9%) within 90 days.

In the CSDH subgroup (n=82), patients receiving adjunct MMA embolisation (n=18) had a lower 90-day recurrence rate than those managed with burr-hole drainage alone (n=64): 8.3% (1/18) versus 25.0% (16/64), respectively (p=0.04). This direction of effect, while based on small numbers, aligns with emerging evidence from other centres.10

On multivariate logistic regression (Table 2), the strongest independent predictors of unfavourable outcome were bilateral fixed dilated pupils (AOR 12.1; 95% CI 5.6 to 26.2; p<0.001), basal cistern obliteration (AOR 9.8; 95% CI 4.4 to 21.8; p<0.001), GCS ≤8 at admission (AOR 8.4; 95% CI 4.2 to 16.8; p<0.001), midline shift >10 mm (AOR 6.3; 95% CI 3.1 to 12.8; p<0.001), and surgery beyond 4 hours post-injury in acute SDH (AOR 3.6; 95% CI 1.8 to 7.2; p=0.003).1,2,8,17,18 MMA embolisation as an adjunct in CSDH was associated with lower odds of unfavourable outcome (AOR 0.35; 95% CI 0.10 to 0.92; p=0.04).10 Chronic SDH subtype was protective compared with acute SDH (AOR 0.18; 95% CI 0.09 to 0.37; p<0.001).4,7,20

The model showed adequate fit (Hosmer–Lemeshow χ²=6.82, df=8, p=0.557) with Nagelkerke pseudo-R² of 0.72. All VIFs were <2.5, indicating no meaningful multicollinearity. Age >80 years was associated with higher odds of unfavourable outcome (AOR 2.40; 95% CI 1.30 to 4.40; p=0.005), but intermediate age groups and sex did not reach significance.

Discussion

In our study, road traffic accidents were the leading cause of SDH (43.7%), which is typical for our region where young males on two-wheelers are frequently involved. This is quite different from Western countries where falls in elderly patients on blood thinners are more common.5,14 The male preponderance (78.1%) and the relatively younger mean age (48.6 years) in our cohort reflect the reality of South Indian trauma epidemiology. Our 30-day mortality of 20.0% is on the higher side compared with the 10–15% reported from Western acute SDH series,2,8 and we think this largely reflects the combination of delayed presentation and a higher proportion of severe acute SDH from high-velocity crashes reaching our centre.

GCS at admission (AOR 8.4 for GCS ≤8) and bilateral fixed pupils (AOR 12.1) were the two strongest clinical predictors in our model, consistent with Servadei et al17 and Dent et al.18 A low GCS at triage typically reflects either direct cortical compression, associated diffuse axonal injury, or secondary brainstem compression — all of which limit recovery potential. Bilateral fixed pupils indicate transtentorial herniation with third nerve compression, and in our experience these patients rarely achieved favourable outcomes regardless of how quickly we proceeded to surgery. The practical value of these two assessments is that both can be performed at the bedside without specialised equipment, making them applicable even in peripheral referral hospitals that send patients to centres like ours.

Among imaging parameters, basal cistern obliteration (AOR 9.8) was a stronger predictor than midline shift >10 mm (AOR 6.3) or haematoma volume >60 mL (AOR 6.3). Cistern status can be quickly assessed on the initial non-contrast CT without volume calculations, and it correlates with herniation severity more directly than linear shift alone, as also described by Parizel et al.12 In our daily practice, we now routinely flag cistern status on the CT report as a rapid prognostic indicator for the neurosurgery team.

The finding that surgery beyond 4 hours was associated with higher odds of unfavourable outcome (AOR 3.6) in acute SDH is consistent with Seelig et al's 1981 observation1 and the subsequent guidelines by Bullock et al.8 But here is the practical problem we face: the median time from injury to arrival at our centre was approximately 6.5 hours. Most of these patients were first seen at a peripheral hospital, stabilised there, and then referred — the delay happened before they ever reached our emergency department. Simply improving in-hospital response time would not fix this. What would help is strengthening ambulance networks and establishing direct transfer protocols so that severe head injury patients bypass intermediate hospitals and reach a tertiary centre faster.

MMA embolisation as an adjunct to burr-hole drainage appeared protective in our CSDH patients (AOR 0.35), with a lower recurrence rate of 8.3% (1/18) versus 25.0% (16/64) in those managed with drainage alone at 90 days (p=0.04). We acknowledge the numbers are small (n=18 in the embolisation group), but the direction of effect matches what Link et al10 and others have reported. The practical limitation at our centre is that interventional neuroradiology services are not available round-the-clock, so we could not offer MMA embolisation to every eligible CSDH patient. Whether this approach should become standard practice in LMIC settings needs to be evaluated in properly powered randomised trials.

Our overall CSDH recurrence rate of 20.9% falls within the 5–33% range reported in Almenawer et al's meta-analysis of over 34,000 patients.16 We noticed that recurrence was lower in the latter part of our study period, which we attribute to two changes: routine subdural drain placement following Santarius et al's trial,6 and increasing — though not universal — use of MMA embolisation. Brennan et al reported lower recurrence rates in a UK multicentre cohort,23 and the difference probably reflects variation in surgical technique, drain duration, and postoperative protocols across centres.

A few centre-specific challenges shaped our results. First, the high proportion of RTA patients transferred after variable delays meant that many had already exceeded the 4-hour window on arrival. Second, approximately 40% of our patients lacked health insurance, which affected discharge planning and follow-up compliance, contributing to our 4.4% loss to follow-up. Third, interventional neuroradiology access was limited to working hours, constraining our ability to offer timely MMA embolisation. These challenges are not unique to our centre but reflect the broader reality of emergency neurosurgical care in public hospitals across South Asia.

Limitations

The single-centre design limits generalisability, particularly to private hospital settings or other regions within India. The retrospective record review component introduces risk of information bias, especially for time-dependent variables like time from injury to surgery. We lost 12 patients (4.4%) to 30-day follow-up, which, while modest, could have affected outcome proportions. The MMA embolisation subgroup was small (n=18), and our findings regarding this intervention should be considered hypothesis-generating. We did not perform formal inter-rater reliability assessments for imaging measurements, which may introduce variability. Finally, the observational design means we can report associations but not causation — though the existing randomised evidence for the 4-hour window is supportive.1

Conclusion

In this prospective South Indian cohort, GCS at admission and bilateral fixed pupils were the dominant independent predictors of unfavourable 30-day outcome, while basal cistern obliteration appeared to be the strongest imaging predictor of mortality. These bedside and CT-based assessments — which require no specialised technology — may serve as practical triage tools in resource-limited emergency settings. A streamlined 4-hour surgical pathway and adjunctive MMA embolisation for chronic SDH may improve outcomes, but these findings require confirmation in larger multicentre or randomised studies conducted in LMIC settings.
