Scope & Purpose

Rapid bedside reference for intensivists, residents, and respiratory therapists

This document synthesizes landmark trials, international society guidelines, and emerging 2026 recommendations for mechanical ventilation in adult ICU patients. Content spans from the foundational 1960s physiology through the 2000 ARDSNet revolution to contemporary precision-medicine approaches including driving pressure titration, esophageal manometry, and individualized PEEP strategies.

💡
Key Principle: Mechanical ventilation is supportive, not curative. The primary goal is to maintain adequate gas exchange while minimizing ventilator-induced lung injury (VILI) until the underlying process resolves.
✅
Lung-Protective Paradigm: Low tidal volume (4–8 mL/kg PBW), plateau pressure <30 cmH₂O, appropriate PEEP, and prone positioning for moderate-severe ARDS.

● Historical Evolution of Ventilation Strategy

1963

Bendixen — The High-VT Era

Bendixen demonstrated that tidal volumes of 10–15 mL/kg during anesthesia reduced atelectasis and improved oxygenation. This established the standard for the next 35 years.

1967

Ashbaugh et al. — ARDS Described

First clinical description of acute respiratory distress syndrome in 12 patients with refractory hypoxemia, decreased compliance, and diffuse bilateral infiltrates.

1990–1992

Hickling — Permissive Hypercapnia

Introduction of the concept that limiting airway pressure and accepting hypercapnia ("permissive hypercapnia") could improve survival in ARDS.

1998

Amato et al. — Protective Ventilation RCT

Small RCT (n=53) suggesting mortality benefit from low tidal volume ventilation combined with PEEP above the lower inflection point.

2000

ARDSNet ARMA Trial

Landmark multicenter RCT (n=861) establishing 6 mL/kg PBW as standard. In-hospital mortality reduced from 39.8% to 31.0% (P=0.007).

2003

Cochrane Review

Systematic review confirming that lung-protective strategies (low tidal volume + limited pressure) improve survival in ARDS.

2017

ATS/ESICM/SCCM Guidelines

Official clinical practice guideline strongly recommending low tidal volumes (4–8 mL/kg PBW), limiting plateau pressure, and prone positioning for severe ARDS (>12 h/d).

2018

FICM/ICS ARDS Guideline (UK)

UK guidance endorsing low tidal volumes (<6 mL/kg), prone positioning, and conservative fluid management. Against routine HFOV.

2023

ESICM Practice Guidelines

Strong recommendation for low VT ventilation. Against prolonged high-pressure recruitment maneuvers. Conditional recommendation for higher PEEP.

2024–2026

Precision Medicine Era

ATS 2024 update on ARDS; ERS 2025 SPARC study on protective vs spontaneous breathing; SCCM 2026 NMBA guidelines emphasizing individualized care.

● Lung-Protective Ventilation Strategy

💪

Core Physiology: The Baby Lung

ARDS affects the lung heterogeneously — only a small fraction ("baby lung") remains aerated and participates in gas exchange.

⚠
VILI Mechanisms: Volutrauma (overdistention), Barotrauma (alveolar rupture from pressure), Atelectrauma (cyclic recruitment-derecruitment), and Biotrauma (inflammatory mediator release). All are mitigated by lung-protective strategy.

Tidal Volume

Target: 4–8 mL/kg PBW
PBW Male = 50 + 0.91(height cm − 152.4)
PBW Female = 45.5 + 0.91(height cm − 152.4)

ARDSNet target is 6 mL/kg PBW. May reduce to 4 mL/kg if plateau pressure >30 cmH₂O. May increase to 8 mL/kg if Pplat <25 and dyspnea is severe.

Plateau Pressure

Goal: ≤30 cmH₂O
Measure with 0.5s inspiratory pause
Check q4h and after PEEP/VT changes

Plateau pressure is a surrogate for stress applied to the "baby lung." If >30, decrease VT by 1 mL/kg steps (minimum 4 mL/kg). If <25 and VT <6, may increase VT.

Driving Pressure

ΔP = Pplat − PEEP
Target: <14 cmH₂O
Surrogate for lung strain / compliance

Driving pressure (plateau minus PEEP) reflects the dynamic strain on functional lung tissue. Amato et al. (2015) showed driving pressure is the variable most strongly associated with survival.

● ARDSNet Ventilation Protocol

⚖

Ventilator Setup & Adjustment

Based on NIH ARDS Network ARMA trial (2000) and subsequent updates

Initial Settings

  1. Calculate PBW (not actual body weight)
  2. Select any mode (typically AC-VC or AC-PC)
  3. Set initial VT = 8 mL/kg PBW
  4. Reduce VT by 1 mL/kg q≤2h until 6 mL/kg PBW
  5. Set RR to approximate baseline MV (max 35/min)
  6. Set minimum PEEP = 5 cmH₂O

Oxygenation Goal

🎯
PaO₂ 55–80 mmHg or SpO₂ 88–95%
Avoid hyperoxemia; use lowest FiO₂ necessary

pH Management

Goal pH 7.30–7.45. Permissive hypercapnia is acceptable.

  • pH 7.15–7.30: Increase RR to 35
  • pH <7.15: Increase RR to 35; if still acidotic, increase VT in 1 mL/kg steps until pH >7.15 (Pplat target may be exceeded temporarily)
  • Consider NaHCO₃ if hemodynamically unstable

● Ventilator Modes & Waveforms

Controlled

Volume Control (VC-AC / VC-CMV)

Delivers preset tidal volume at set rate. Every triggered breath receives the full VT. Peak pressure varies with compliance/resistance.

  • VT: 4–8 mL/kg PBW
  • RR: 12–35 (adjust for pH)
  • Flow: 40–60 L/min
  • I:E ratio: 1:2 to 1:3
Controlled

Pressure Control (PC-AC / PC-CMV)

Delivers preset inspiratory pressure. VT varies with compliance, resistance, and patient effort. Better gas distribution in non-homogeneous lungs.

  • Pi: 10–20 cmH₂O above PEEP
  • Ti: ~1 second (I:E 1:2 to 1:3)
  • Monitor VT closely
  • Risk of hypoventilation if compliance falls
Dual

PRVC / VC+ / AutoFlow

Pressure-regulated volume control: targets set VT with lowest possible pressure. Adapts breath-by-breath to changing mechanics.

  • Set target VT and max pressure limit
  • Ventilator adjusts inspiratory pressure
  • Combines VT control with pressure benefits
  • Good for varying compliance
Combined

SIMV + Pressure Support

Mandatory breaths at set rate synchronized to patient effort. Spontaneous breaths above the set rate receive pressure support.

  • Mandatory VT per control setting
  • PS: 5–15 cmH₂O for spontaneous
  • Useful for weaning
  • Monitor spontaneous VT and RR
Spontaneous

Pressure Support (PSV) / CPAP

Patient initiates all breaths; ventilator delivers preset pressure above PEEP. Used for weaning and non-invasive ventilation.

  • PS: 5–15 cmH₂O (wean to 5–8)
  • PEEP: 5 cmH₂O minimum
  • No set RR (backup apnea ventilation)
  • Monitor for respiratory fatigue
Rescue

APRV (Airway Pressure Release)

Spontaneous breathing at Phigh with brief releases to Plow. Maintains recruitment while allowing unrestricted spontaneous effort.

  • Phigh: ~20–30 cmH₂O
  • Plow: 0 cmH₂O
  • Thigh: 4–6 seconds; Tlow: 0.2–0.8s
  • Contraindicated if intracranial hypertension

● ARDS Management: 2026 Updates

🌟

SCCM 2026 Guidelines: Neuromuscular Blockade in ARDS

Published April 2026 · Critical Care Medicine

The Society of Critical Care Medicine released updated guidance on neuromuscular blocking agents (NMBAs) in adults with ARDS, shifting toward more individualized, conditional recommendations:

RecommendationStrengthEvidenceClinical Implication
Use NMBAs in adults with ARDS (PaO₂/FiO₂ <150) who are persistently hypoxemic and/or not achieving lung-protective targets on sedation optimization Conditional For Low Emphasize ventilator/sedation optimization before NMBA. Limit duration; do not routinely exceed 48 hours.
Fixed-dose vs titration-based NMBA strategy (monitoring depth with TOF) Conditional For Very Low Either strategy acceptable; institutional protocolization recommended.
Scale-based vs non-scale evaluation of analgesia/sedation depth before NMBA Conditional For Very Low RASS, SAS, or clinical assessment all reasonable.
Monitoring-based vs no monitoring of sedation depth during NMBA infusion Conditional For Very Low Consider EEG or clinical monitoring if paralyzed for >24h.
NMBA during proning — administer or withhold Conditional For either Very Low Individualize based on dyssynchrony, tolerance, and institutional practice.
💡
Key Shift from 2017: The 2017 ATS guidelines strongly recommended against routine continuous NMBA infusion. The 2026 SCCM update suggests NMBAs may be beneficial in selected severely hypoxemic patients (P/F <150) after sedation optimization, reflecting a nuanced, personalized approach.

ATS 2024 Update Highlights

  • PEEP strategy remains uncertain: No single optimal PEEP strategy; consider recruitability, hemodynamics, and driving pressure.
  • Higher PEEP without recruitment maneuvers suggested in moderate-severe ARDS.
  • Esophageal pressure monitoring and EIT may guide individualized PEEP.
  • Prone positioning: Strong recommendation maintained for PaO₂/FiO₂ <150, >12–16 hours daily.
  • Conservative fluid strategy suggested once shock resolved.

ERS 2025 — SPARC Study

Preclinical data presented at ERS Congress 2025 (Menga et al.) compared three strategies in ARDS:

  1. Unassisted spontaneous breathing (PSILI model)
  2. CPAP alone
  3. Protective volume-control ventilation

Findings suggest protective mechanical ventilation may be superior to both CPAP and unassisted breathing in severe lung injury, with histological injury markers strongest in the pleural region during spontaneous effort.

● Bedside Monitoring & Targets

📈

Gas Exchange

  • SpO₂: 88–95% (avoid >96% hyperoxemia)
  • PaO₂: 55–80 mmHg
  • PaCO₂: Allow permissive hypercapnia
  • pH: 7.30–7.45 (acceptable 7.15–7.30)
  • EtCO₂: Trend monitoring; ABG correlation
📐

Mechanics

  • Plateau Pressure: ≤30 cmH₂O
  • Driving Pressure: <14 cmH₂O
  • PEEP: 5–24 cmH₂O (per ARDSNet)
  • Respiratory System Compliance: Trend
  • Mechanical Power: <17 J/min (emerging)
💪

Hemodynamics

  • MAP >65 mmHg (individualize)
  • Right Ventricular function assessment
  • Fluid responsiveness: PPV, SVV (caution with low VT)
  • Lactate clearance
  • Capillary refill time (microcirculation)
🔧

Advanced Monitoring: Driving Pressure & Transpulmonary Pressure

Moving beyond simple plateau pressure toward precision medicine

Driving Pressure (ΔP) = Pplat − PEEP
Normal <10–12 cmH₂O · ARDS Target <14 cmH₂O

Driving pressure is the tidal strain normalized to functional lung size (compliance). It is the strongest predictor of survival in ARDS, stronger than VT or plateau pressure alone. If ΔP >14 despite VT 6 mL/kg, consider:

  • Prone positioning
  • Recruitment maneuvers (brief, cautious)
  • Esophageal manometry for transpulmonary pressure
  • ECMO evaluation if refractory
Transpulmonary Pressure (PL) = Paw − Pes
End-inspiratory PL <20–25 cmH₂O
End-expiratory PL 0–2 cmH₂O (avoid collapse)

Esophageal manometry estimates pleural pressure (Pes), allowing calculation of actual lung-distending pressure. This is especially valuable in:

  • Obesity (elevated pleural pressure)
  • Chest wall stiffness (burns, edema)
  • Prone positioning optimization
  • Setting PEEP to overcome collapse pressure

● Weaning & Ventilator Liberation

🏆

ATS/CHEST 2017 Liberation Guidelines

Still the foundational framework for ventilator discontinuation

Readiness Criteria (Daily Assessment)

✅
Evidence of disease reversal
PaO₂/FiO₂ >150–200 on PEEP ≤5–8, FiO₂ ≤0.4–0.5
pH ≥7.25, Hemodynamic stability
Spontaneous inspiratory effort present

Additional useful criteria: Afebrile, Hgb >8–10 g/dL, adequate mentation, stable electrolytes, no significant acidosis.

Spontaneous Breathing Trial (SBT)

For patients ventilated >24 hours, initial SBT should use inspiratory pressure augmentation (5–8 cmH₂O) rather than T-piece or CPAP alone.

  • Duration: 30–120 minutes
  • Assess: RR, HR, BP, SpO₂, accessory muscle use, diaphoresis, dyspnea
  • Pass → Assess airway protection & secretions → Extubate
  • Fail → Identify cause → Comfortable support → Retry in 24h

● Quick Reference: Ventilator Settings

Initial ARDS Setup

Mode: AC-VC or AC-PC
VT: 6 mL/kg PBW
RR: ~20–35 (match MV)
PEEP: 5 cmH₂O min
FiO₂: 1.0 → titrate to SpO₂ 88–95%
I:E: 1:2 to 1:3
Plateau: ≤30 cmH₂O
Driving P: <14 cmH₂O

Troubleshooting Hypoxemia

1. Check tube position / secretions
2. Increase PEEP per table
3. Prone if P/F <150
4. Consider recruitment (brief)
5. Inhaled NO (rescue)
6. Evaluate for pneumothorax
7. ECMO consult if refractory
Avoid: High VT, high Pplat, routine HFOV

Troubleshooting Hypercapnia

1. Increase RR (max 35)
2. Check for circuit leaks
3. Reduce dead space if possible
4. Accept permissive hypercapnia
5. If pH <7.15: increase VT in 1 mL/kg steps even if Pplat >30 temporarily
6. Consider NaHCO₃ if unstable
7. Evaluate for mucus plugging
