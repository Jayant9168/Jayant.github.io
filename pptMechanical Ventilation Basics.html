Fundamentals

Core Principles

Mechanical ventilation in the ICU has evolved from simple volume delivery to precision lung-protective strategies that minimize ventilator-induced lung injury (VILI) while optimizing gas exchange and patient-ventilator synchrony.

Lung Protection

Minimize volutrauma, barotrauma, atelectrauma, and biotrauma through low tidal volumes (4–8 mL/kg PBW), limited plateau pressures (<30 cmH₂O), and appropriate PEEP selection.

Oxygenation Targets

Modern strategies target conservative SpO₂ (88–94% in ARDS) to avoid hyperoxemia-related mortality. Titrate FiO₂ to the minimum necessary level while maintaining adequate tissue oxygen delivery.

Patient-Ventilator Synchrony

Optimize trigger sensitivity, inspiratory flow, and cycling criteria to reduce work of breathing, prevent patient-ventilator dyssynchrony, and minimize sedation requirements.

Driving Pressure

ΔP = Pplat − PEEP. Emerging as a key predictor of survival in ARDS. Target ≤15 cmH₂O. Reflects the dynamic strain applied to the functional lung (the "baby lung").

Ventilator Modes

Mode Waveform Diagrams

Visual comparison of pressure, volume, and flow patterns across the four fundamental ICU ventilation modes.

Volume Control (VCV / VAC)

Constant flow, variable pressure. Square pressure waveform with decelerating flow. Guaranteed Vt regardless of compliance changes.

Pressure Control (PCV / PAC)

Decelerating flow, constant pressure. Lower peak pressures (Ppeak = Pplat). Vt varies with lung compliance and resistance.

Pressure Support (PSV)

Patient-triggered, pressure-limited, flow-cycled. Each breath initiated by patient. Used for weaning and spontaneous breathing trials.

SIMV + Pressure Support

Mandatory breaths synchronized to patient effort. Spontaneous breaths between mandatory cycles receive pressure support. Useful for weaning.

Evidence Evolution

Guidelines Timeline: Old vs. New

From traditional liberal ventilation to precision lung-protective and driving-pressure-guided strategies.

Pre-2000

Traditional Ventilation Era

Tidal volumes of 10–15 mL/kg actual body weight were standard. Plateau pressures often exceeded 35–45 cmH₂O. Focus was on normalization of blood gases rather than lung protection.

Historical Standard
2000

ARDSNet ARMA Trial

Landmark RCT (n=861) demonstrating 6 mL/kg PBW vs. 12 mL/kg reduced mortality from 40% to 31% (NNT=11). Established low tidal volume ventilation as the standard of care for ARDS.

NEJM 2000;342:1301-8
2012

Berlin Definition of ARDS

Revised ARDS definition replaced the older AECC criteria. Introduced severity stratification (mild, moderate, severe) based on PaO₂/FiO₂ ratio with minimum PEEP ≥5 cmH₂O.

JAMA 2012;307:2526-33
2017

ATS/ESICM/SCCM Guideline

Strong recommendation for low tidal volumes (4–8 mL/kg PBW) and plateau pressures <30 cmH₂O. Strong recommendation for prone positioning >12 h/day in severe ARDS. Conditional recommendation for higher PEEP and recruitment maneuvers.

Am J Respir Crit Care Med 2017;195:1253-63
2023

ESICM Practice Guidelines Update

Strong recommendation for low tidal volume (4–8 mL/kg PBW). Strong recommendation against prolonged high-pressure recruitment maneuvers. Weak recommendation against brief recruitment maneuvers. Strong recommendation for prone positioning in moderate-severe ARDS.

Intensive Care Med 2023
2023–2024

Global ARDS Definition Consensus

Expanded ARDS definition to include patients on HFNC (≥30 L/min) and NIV with equivalent PEEP. Supported SpO₂/FiO₂ ratio for oxygenation assessment and lung ultrasound for diagnosis in resource-limited settings.

ESICM 2023 / Global Consensus 2024
2024–2026

Precision Ventilation Era

Current focus on driving pressure (ΔP ≤15 cmH₂O), mechanical power limitation, personalized PEEP titration, and avoidance of routine neuromuscular blockade. Emphasis on conservative oxygen targets and early mobilization.

Liberation

Weaning & Extubation

Systematic approach to ventilator liberation with evidence-based readiness criteria and spontaneous breathing trial protocols.

Readiness Criteria

  • ✓ Resolution of acute insult / clinically stable
  • ✓ FiO₂ ≤ 40–50% with PEEP ≤ 8–10 cmH₂O
  • ✓ Hemodynamically stable (minimal/no vasopressors)
  • ✓ RASS ≥ −3 (awake and follow commands)
  • ✓ Cough strength adequate; manageable secretions

Spontaneous Breathing Trial

Daily SBTs are the standard of care. Preferred methods:

  • 1. Low-level PSV (5–8 cmH₂O) with PEEP 5
  • 2. T-piece trial for 30–120 minutes
  • 3. CPAP 5 cmH₂O trial
  • Pass criteria: RR <30, SpO₂ >90%, no distress, stable hemodynamics

Post-Extubation Support

Prophylactic strategies for high-risk patients:

  • • HFNC (Optiflow) immediately post-extubation
  • • NIV for hypercapnic respiratory failure risk
  • • Cough assistance and early mobilization
  • • Consider prophylactic tracheostomy in selected prolonged cases

Weaning Failure Predictors

  • ✗ Rapid shallow breathing index (RSBI) >105
  • ✗ Respiratory rate >30 during SBT
  • ✗ Heart rate >140 or change >20%
  • ✗ Systolic BP >180 or <90 mmHg
  • ✗ Diaphoresis, anxiety, or deteriorating gas exchange
