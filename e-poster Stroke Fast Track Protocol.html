Stroke Classification & Epidemiology

Understanding stroke subtypes is critical for appropriate acute management

Figure 1. Global stroke distribution by pathophysiological subtype
Ischemic Stroke (85%)
Caused by arterial occlusion from thrombus or embolus, leading to irreversible neuronal injury within minutes. Core infarct surrounded by ischemic penumbra (salvageable tissue). Key subtypes: Large Vessel Occlusion (LVO), Small Vessel Disease, Cardioembolic, Cryptogenic.

LVO: 24–46% of ischemic strokes Mortality: 8–12% at 30 days
Hemorrhagic Stroke (15%)
Results from rupture of blood vessels causing direct tissue damage and mass effect. Includes intracerebral hemorrhage (ICH) and subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH). Higher early mortality than ischemic stroke.

ICH: 10–15% of all strokes SAH: 5% of all strokes Mortality: 40–50% at 30 days

Time is Brain — Quantified

Every 1 minute of untreated large vessel stroke results in loss of 1.9 million neurons, 13.8 billion synapses, and 7.5 miles of myelinated fibers. For every 15-minute reduction in door-to-needle time, there is a 4% greater odds of walking independently at discharge and 4% lower odds of death before discharge.

EMS Prehospital Care
  • Obtain last known well time and witness contact
  • Establish IV access, cardiac monitoring, O2 if SpO2 < 94%
  • Check blood glucose; treat hypoglycemia if < 60 mg/dL
  • Determine symptom onset using Cincinnati or LAPSS
  • Prenotify receiving hospital (Code Stroke)
  • Transport to nearest Certified Stroke Center
  • Avoid unnecessary delays — do not wait for family
Hospital Triage (Level I)
  • Immediate bed placement in ED resuscitation bay
  • Activate Stroke Team with single phone call/page
  • Obtain vital signs, continuous pulse oximetry
  • Establish 2 large-bore IV lines (18G or larger)
  • Draw labs: CBC, CMP, coagulation panel, troponin
  • 12-lead ECG within 10 minutes of arrival
  • Target door-to-physician evaluation ≤ 10 minutes

EMS Prenotification Evidence

EMS prenotification of the receiving hospital significantly enhances evaluation and treatment metrics. Studies show shorter door-to-imaging times (26 vs 31 minutes), increased IV tPA administration rates within recommended windows (82.8% vs 79.2%), and improved 90-day functional outcomes (mRS 0–2: 52% vs 46%). Prenotification allows pre-mixing of thrombolytic and team assembly before patient arrival.

IV Thrombolysis Protocol

Alteplase and Tenecteplase administration guidelines

Inclusion Criteria
  • Disabling acute ischemic stroke within 4.5h of symptom onset
  • Age ≥ 18 years (pediatric use off-label)
  • Measurable neurological deficit (NIHSS ≥ 1, not rapidly improving)
  • No hemorrhage on non-contrast head CT
  • Blood pressure ≤ 185/110 mmHg (treatable if elevated)
  • Blood glucose > 50 mg/dL (treat hypoglycemia first)
  • INR ≤ 1.7 (if on warfarin) or not on DOACs
  • No prior intracranial hemorrhage
  • No major surgery or trauma within 14 days
  • No GI or GU bleeding within 21 days
  • No arterial puncture at non-compressible site within 7 days
Absolute Contraindications
  • Active internal bleeding
  • Intracranial hemorrhage on CT
  • Known intracranial vascular malformation
  • Known intracranial neoplasm (primary or metastatic)
  • Ischemic stroke within 3 months
  • Intracranial or spinal surgery within 3 months
  • Suspected aortic dissection
  • Active bleeding diathesis
  • Platelet count < 100,000/mm³
  • Seizure at onset with postictal residual impairments

AHA/ASA 2026 Update: Tenecteplase Endorsement

The 2026 AHA/ASA guidelines now endorse either alteplase or tenecteplase for eligible patients within the 4.5-hour window. Tenecteplase offers practical advantages: single bolus administration, reduced medication errors, no infusion pump requirement, and potentially faster door-to-needle times. The AcT trial (N=1600) demonstrated non-inferiority for functional outcome at 90 days (mRS 0–1: 36.9% vs 34.8%).

Endovascular Thrombectomy (EVT)

Mechanical reperfusion for large vessel occlusion — the standard of care

EVT Selection Criteria
  • Anterior circulation proximal LVO (ICA terminus, M1 segment)
  • NIHSS ≥ 6 (or disabling deficit if NIHSS < 6)
  • Pre-stroke modified Rankin Scale (mRS) 0–1
  • ASPECTS score ≥ 3 on NCCT (or core < 70 mL on CTP)
  • Within 6 hours: Standard indication (DAWN, DEFUSE 3)
  • 6–24 hours: Selected patients with favorable imaging profile
  • Basilar artery occlusion: Up to 24 hours with severe deficits
  • Age ≥ 18 years (selected elderly patients may benefit)
IMAGING SELECTION

CTA confirms LVO. CTP or MRI DWI assesses core/penumbra mismatch for extended window (6–24h). Collateral status on CTA predicts response.

Procedure & Post-Procedure Care
  • Femoral or radial arterial access under local anesthesia
  • Stent retriever (Solitaire, Trevo) or aspiration (ACE, Sofia)
  • Combined stent retriever + aspiration (Solumbra/ADVANCE)
  • Target: TICI 2b/3 reperfusion (≥ 50% territory)
  • IV tPA should NOT delay EVT if indicated
  • Post-procedure: ICU monitoring, BP < 180/105
  • Repeat CT at 24h or sooner if clinical deterioration
  • Start antiplatelet after 24h if no hemorrhage on follow-up CT
TICI 2b-3: Target reperfusion mTICI 2c/3: Ideal outcome

EVT Evidence Summary

Meta-analysis of 9 RCTs (MR CLEAN, ESCAPE, EXTEND-IA, SWIFT PRIME, REVASCAT, DAWN, DEFUSE 3, AURORA, TESLA) demonstrates EVT plus medical therapy vs medical therapy alone: Number Needed to Treat (NNT) = 2.6 for functional independence (mRS 0–2) at 90 days. Absolute risk reduction for mRS 0–2: 24%. Mortality reduction: 4.5% absolute. Symptomatic ICH rate: ~4.4%.

NIH Stroke Scale (NIHSS)

Standardized quantification of neurological deficit severity

NIHSS Overview
The NIHSS is a 15-item neurological examination scale used to evaluate acute stroke severity. Score range: 0–42. Higher scores indicate more severe deficits. Trained providers (physicians, nurses, paramedics) must complete certification for reliable scoring.

Critical thresholds:
  • NIHSS ≥ 6: Strongly consider EVT if LVO present
  • NIHSS > 22: High risk for hemorrhagic conversion
  • NIHSS 0 with symptoms: Possible TIA — rapid workup

Post-Acute Stroke Care

Stroke unit admission, secondary prevention, and rehabilitation

Stroke Unit Admission
  • Admit to dedicated stroke unit with telemetry
  • Neuro checks every 15 min × 2h, then q30min × 6h, then q1h × 16h
  • Blood pressure management per protocol
  • Swallow screen before any oral intake
  • DVT prophylaxis (LMWH or SCD) within 48h
  • Early mobilization within 24–48 hours if stable
  • Nutrition assessment and dysphagia management
Secondary Prevention
  • Antiplatelet: Aspirin 81mg daily (start 24h post-tPA if no bleed)
  • Dual antiplatelet: Aspirin + Clopidogrel × 21–90 days (minor stroke/TIA)
  • Anticoagulation: For AF (DOAC preferred over warfarin)
  • Statin: High-intensity (Atorvastatin 40–80mg or Rosuvastatin 20–40mg)
  • BP control: Target < 130/80 mmHg
  • Diabetes: Target HbA1c < 7%
  • Smoking cessation, diet, exercise counseling
Rehabilitation
  • Physical therapy evaluation within 24h of admission
  • Occupational therapy for ADL assessment
  • Speech-language pathology for aphasia/dysphagia
  • Early supported discharge (ESD) teams when appropriate
  • Constraint-induced movement therapy for hemiparesis
  • Robotic-assisted gait training
  • Community reintegration and caregiver training

Complications to Monitor

Neurological: Hemorrhagic transformation, cerebral edema/malignant MCA infarction, seizure (5–10%), stroke recurrence (3–5% at 30 days).
Medical: Aspiration pneumonia (15–25%), DVT/PE (2–5%), urinary tract infection, pressure injuries, cardiac arrhythmias.
Post-stroke depression: Occurs in 30–50% of survivors — screen with PHQ-9 at 2 weeks and 3 months.
